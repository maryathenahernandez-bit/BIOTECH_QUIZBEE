🧬 What is Biotechnology?

Biotechnology is the use of living organisms or their products to improve life and solve problems.

This includes using parts of living things such as cells, enzymes, or genes to make useful products for people, animals, and the environment. It is an interdisciplinary science connecting biology, chemistry, genetics, and technology.

📌 Key Characteristics

  • Uses living organisms to produce useful products
  • Connects biology, chemistry, genetics, and technology
  • Improves health, agriculture, and the environment

🌾 Everyday Examples

  • Genetically modified (GM) foods
  • Crops that resist pests or are more nutritious
  • Life-saving medicines and vaccines
  • Eco-friendly fuels

🌈 7 Types of Biotechnology

1️⃣ Microbial Biotechnology

Uses bacteria, yeast, fungi. Applications: enzymes, vaccines, insulin, HGH, bioremediation, food safety detection.

2️⃣ Agricultural Biotechnology

Pest-resistant crops, nutrient-enriched foods, molecular pharming (e.g., tobacco for medicines), biofuels. Global food production must increase ~70% by 2050.

3️⃣ Animal Biotechnology

Transgenic animals for protein production. 1997: Dolly the sheep cloned via nucleus transfer — first mammal cloned this way.

4️⃣ Forensic Biotechnology

DNA fingerprinting: identify individuals, solve crimes, prove innocence, confirm paternity, trace disease outbreaks, protect endangered species.

5️⃣ Bioremediation

Uses bacteria, fungi, plants to clean polluted environments. Examples: 1989 Exxon Valdez oil spill; 2010 Deepwater Horizon — microbial enzymes degraded oil.

6️⃣ Aquatic Biotechnology

Aquaculture provides nearly half of world's seafood. Transgenic salmon grow faster. Bioprospecting marine organisms for novel medicines and enzymes.

7️⃣ Medical Biotechnology

Cancer research, genetic disorder diagnosis, gene therapy, stem cell technology (regenerative medicine), personalized medicine via Human Genome Project.

🔭 Biological Challenges of the 21st Century

Why the "Biotechnology Century"? Genetic and molecular discoveries are reshaping science, transforming how we understand and treat diseases, and applying biotechnological tools to pressing health challenges.

The Human Genome Project (1990–2003) revealed humans have fewer genes than expected, mapped all human genes, and provided insights into gene control of life processes. It opened a new era of personalized medicine and understanding of the proteome (all proteins made by genes).

💊 Pharmacogenomics

Using patient's genetic profile for treatment design. SNPs (Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms) explain why drugs work differently. Simple DNA test could select best medication with fewer side effects. Already applied in cancer treatment — targeted therapies instead of traditional chemotherapy.

🧬 Gene Therapy

Replace or repair defective genes. Challenges in safe delivery to human cells. Successful trials in blood diseases; promising for nervous system diseases. Potential to treat genetic disorders at their root cause.

🔬 Nanotechnology in Medicine

Microscopic drug delivery tools (nanovesicles). Deliver drugs directly to diseased cells. Improved precision, reduced damage to healthy tissues, enhanced treatment effectiveness.

🫀 Stem Cell Technology

Can develop into skin, nerve, or muscle cells. Replace damaged organs, repair tissues, treat paralysis, heart disease, diabetes. Induced Pluripotent Stem Cells (iPSCs) created from adult tissue without destroying embryos.

🌟 Future Promise

  • Many incurable diseases may be treated or prevented
  • Healthier lives and extended life expectancy
  • Treatments tailored to individual genetic makeup
  • Redefined human health standards

The biotechnology century will advance medicine and transform how humans live healthier, longer lives.

📚 Key Terms

Recombinant DNA Technology

Combining DNA molecules from different sources to form new genetic combinations. Synonymous with gene cloning and genetic engineering.

Gene Cloning

Making identical copies of a particular gene for scientific or medical use.

Genetic Engineering

Altering the genetic makeup of an organism using molecular tools.

Clone

From Greek "twig." In biology: a molecule, cell, or organism that is an exact copy of another.

DNA Ligase

Enzyme that joins DNA fragments by sealing breaks in the backbone. Originally joins Okazaki fragments during replication.

Bacteriophage (Phage)

Virus that infects bacteria. Key model organism in early molecular biology research.

Insert DNA

The foreign DNA fragment introduced into a vector for cloning.

Restriction Site

Specific palindromic DNA sequence recognized and cut by a restriction enzyme.

Palindrome

Sequence that reads the same on complementary strands in the 5'→3' direction. E.g., 5'-GAATTC-3'.

✂️ Restriction Enzymes — "Molecular Scissors"

Restriction enzymes are bacterial proteins that cut DNA at specific restriction sites. They are the molecular scissors of recombinant DNA technology.

Key Properties

  • Defense mechanism: protect bacteria from bacteriophages by cutting foreign DNA
  • Recognize palindromic sequences (4, 6, or 8 bp long) — NOT random
  • Named after source bacterium: EcoRI = E. coli R, enzyme I

Types of Ends Produced

  • Sticky Ends (Cohesive): single-stranded overhangs; produced by EcoRI, BamHI, HindIII, TaqI; PREFERRED for gene cloning
  • Blunt Ends: both strands cut at exact same position; produced by AluI, HaeIII, SmaI; less efficient but more versatile

🔄 The Gene Cloning Process
1
ISOLATE — Isolate plasmid (vector) DNA from bacteria AND the target gene from source organism's DNA.
2
CUT — Cut both DNAs with the SAME restriction enzyme (e.g., EcoRI), producing compatible cohesive ends.
3
MIX & LIGATE — Mix the cut DNAs. Complementary sticky ends join by hydrogen bonding. Add DNA ligase to covalently seal → recombinant plasmid.
4
TRANSFORM — Introduce recombinant plasmids into competent E. coli via heat shock (CaCl₂) or electroporation.
5
SELECT — Plate on ampicillin + X-gal agar. Use blue-white selection to identify white (recombinant) colonies.
6
AMPLIFY — Grow selected white colonies; isolate and purify recombinant plasmid DNA for downstream use.

🔵 Blue-White Selection: "Blue = Boring, White = Winner"

🔵 BLUE Colonies = NOT Desired

  • Plasmid closed WITHOUT insert
  • lacZ gene INTACT → produces β-galactosidase
  • β-gal breaks down X-gal → turns BLUE

⬜ WHITE Colonies = DESIRED ✓

  • Insert DNA disrupts lacZ site
  • lacZ gene DISRUPTED → NO β-galactosidase
  • X-gal NOT broken down → stays WHITE

Note: Ampicillin selection first confirms cells took up SOME plasmid. Blue-white selection identifies which is recombinant. Both are used together.

🚀 Cloning Vectors

Properties of a Good Vector

Small Size (~2–20 kb)

Easy separation from chromosomal DNA and easy manipulation.

Origin of Replication (ori)

Site where replication begins. High-copy plasmids: hundreds to thousands of copies per cell.

Multiple Cloning Site (MCS)

Short region with restriction sites for many enzymes; flexible DNA insertion.

Selectable Marker Genes

Ampicillin (ampR), tetracycline (tetR), lacZ gene — identify transformed cells and recombinant clones.

RNA Polymerase Promoter

Allow transcription of cloned genes in vivo (cell) or in vitro (lab). Essential for protein expression.

DNA Sequencing Primer Sites

5' and 3' primer sites flanking MCS for easy sequencing of inserted fragments.

Special Vector Details

🦠 Bacteriophage Lambda (λ)

Chromosome ~49 kb; engineered to carry inserts up to 25 kb. Has COS sites (cohesive ends) used to circularize DNA during infection. Creates plaques (clear zones of bacterial lysis) on a bacterial lawn. Replicates via the lytic cycle — produces many viral particles.

🧫 BAC (Bacterial Artificial Chromosome)

Derived from bacterial F-factors (fertility factors that control replication). Only 1–2 copies per cell — low copy number ensures stability of large inserts. Critical role in the Human Genome Project.

🍺 YAC (Yeast Artificial Chromosome)

Contains all chromosome elements: telomeres, centromere, origin of replication. Largest inserts of any vector: up to 2 megabases (Mb). Functions like actual chromosomes inside yeast cells.

🌿 Ti Vector (Tumor-Inducing)

From Rhizobium radiobacter (formerly Agrobacterium tumefaciens). Naturally transfers T-DNA into plant genome, causing crown gall disease. Modified to remove harmful auxin synthesis genes for safe use in creating GM crops.

📖 DNA Libraries

Shotgun cloning: random cutting and inserting DNA — thousands of random combinations, hard to find a specific gene. DNA libraries solved this problem.

A DNA library is a collection of cloned DNA fragments from an organism, stored in bacteria or viruses. Preserved long-term; searched repeatedly to find specific genes.

How to Create a cDNA Library

1
Extract mRNA from target tissue (e.g., pancreatic beta cells for insulin gene).
2
Anneal a short oligo-dT primer to the poly-A tail of each mRNA molecule.
3
Use reverse transcriptase (RT) to synthesize single-stranded cDNA from the mRNA template.
4
Digest the mRNA strand with NaOH or RNase to remove the RNA template.
5
Synthesize the second cDNA strand using DNA polymerase and random short primers.
6
Add linkers (short synthetic DNA sequences with restriction sites) to both ends of the cDNA.
7
Digest cDNA + plasmid vector with same restriction enzyme; ligate together using DNA ligase.
8
Transform recombinant plasmids into bacteria. Each colony = one cDNA clone = one expressed gene.

Library Screening: Colony Hybridization

A DNA probe is a short, single-stranded DNA sequence complementary to the target gene. Labeled with radioactive isotopes or fluorescent dyes.

1
Grow recombinant bacterial colonies on agar.
2
Press nylon/nitrocellulose membrane onto plate to transfer colony imprints.
3
Lyse cells with alkaline solution and denature DNA into single strands.
4
Bake or UV-expose membrane to permanently fix single-stranded DNA.
5
Add labeled DNA probe complementary to the target gene.
6
Probe hybridizes (base-pairs) with matching sequences on the membrane.
7
Wash off unbound probe. Detect via autoradiography or fluorescent imaging.
8
Match glowing/dark spots to master plate. Culture positive colonies.

📋 Common Exam Questions & Answers

Q: What are restriction enzymes and why are they called "molecular scissors"?

A: Restriction enzymes are bacterial proteins that recognize specific palindromic DNA sequences (restriction sites) and cut the DNA at or near those sites. They are called molecular scissors because they cut DNA precisely at specific locations — just as scissors cut paper at a specific line.

Q: Why are sticky ends preferred over blunt ends for gene cloning?

A: Sticky ends leave single-stranded overhangs that are complementary to other sticky ends produced by the same enzyme. This makes it easier for the insert and vector to join spontaneously by hydrogen bonding before DNA ligase seals the backbone. Blunt ends can join to any blunt end, making the process less specific and efficient.

Q: Why is blue-white selection needed if ampicillin selection is already used?

A: Ampicillin selection only confirms that a bacterium took up SOME plasmid. It cannot distinguish between bacteria with a recombinant plasmid (with insert) vs. a non-recombinant plasmid (re-circularized without insert). Blue-white selection solves this: white colonies = insert disrupted lacZ = recombinant; blue colonies = intact lacZ = no insert.

Q: What is the difference between a genomic library and a cDNA library?

A: A genomic library contains ALL of an organism's DNA including exons, introns, and non-coding regions. A cDNA library contains only expressed (active) genes, derived from mRNA using reverse transcriptase — no introns, no non-coding regions. The cDNA library is simpler and represents only what a cell is actively using.

Q: Why use a cDNA library to produce a eukaryotic protein in bacteria?

A: Bacteria cannot splice introns — they lack the RNA splicing machinery. If you insert a eukaryotic genomic gene with introns into bacteria, the protein cannot be correctly produced. cDNA is made from mature, processed mRNA (introns already removed), so bacteria can directly read and translate it.

Q: List the essential features of a good cloning vector.

A: (1) Small size for easy manipulation, (2) Origin of replication (ori) for autonomous replication, (3) Multiple cloning site (MCS) for flexible DNA insertion, (4) Selectable marker genes (e.g., ampicillin resistance, lacZ) for identification of transformants, (5) RNA polymerase promoter sequences for gene expression, (6) DNA sequencing primer sites for analyzing inserted sequences.

Gram Stain Technique

The Gram stain is a differential staining technique used to classify bacteria. It uses crystal violet (primary stain) and safranin (counterstain) to distinguish bacteria by their cell wall structure.

🟣 Gram-Positive

  • Stains purple (retains crystal violet)
  • Thick peptidoglycan cell walls
  • No outer membrane
  • Example: Staphylococcus aureus

🩷 Gram-Negative

  • Stains pink (loses crystal violet; picks up safranin)
  • Thin peptidoglycan cell walls
  • Has outer membrane & periplasm
  • Example: Escherichia coli

🔄 Bacterial Transformation
Bacterial Transformation
Bacterial transformation is the process by which bacteria take in DNA from their surrounding environment.

Making Bacterial Cells Competent

Treating bacteria with an ice-cold solution of calcium chloride

Transformation Process

1
Mix recombinant plasmid DNA with chilled competent cells.
2
Place on ice → DNA attaches to cell surface.
3
Apply heat shock (37°C–42°C, ~1 min) → DNA enters cell.
4
Allow cells to recover in nutrient broth → express new genes.

Antibiotic Selection and Recombinant Expression

Only cells that have successfully taken up the plasmid DNA with the resistance gene will survive and form colonies. This process is called antibiotic selection.

The process is called transformation because the bacterial cells are genetically altered to gain new properties.

E. coli transformed with the green fluorescent protein.

Electroporation

Electroporation
Electroporation is another method for introducing DNA into bacterial cells. In this technique, an instrument known as an electroporator delivers a brief electrical pulse to a mixture of bacterial cells and DNA.
🍞 Using Microbes for Everyday Application
Microbial biotechnology uses natural or genetically engineered microorganisms to produce essential products. These microbes play a vital role in food production and in manufacturing medically important recombinant proteins that improve human health.
Microorganisms are used in the production of foods such as bread, yogurt, cheese, beer, and wine.

Cheese Production

Cheese production relies on the enzyme rennin, also called chymosin, which coagulates milk; the rennin gene was cloned and expressed in Aspergillus niger, becoming the first genetically engineered food ingredient approved by the FDA in 1990.

Lactic Acid Bacteria

Lactic acid bacteria such as Lactococcus lactis and Lactobacillus acidophilus ferment milk by converting lactose into lactic acid, giving cheese and yogurt their flavor and texture.

Fermenting Microbes

Fermentation
Fermentation is a key microbial process in which microbes convert sugars into useful products such as lactic acid, ethanol, and carbon dioxide.

💉 Production of Recombinant Insulin in Bacteria
Insulin is a hormone made by the beta cells of the pancreas and regulates blood sugar levels.
When the body cannot produce insulin, a person develops Type I diabetes mellitus, which can cause high blood sugar, high blood pressure, poor blood circulation and nerve damage.

Before vs. After Recombinant DNA Technology

❌ Before rDNA Technology

  • Insulin came from pig and cow
  • It was expensive, inefficient, and caused allergic reactions

✅ After rDNA Technology

  • Humulin introduced into bacteria
  • Faster, safer, and cheaper production

How Bacteria Produce Recombinant Insulin

Human insulin consists of A chain (21 amino acids) and B chain (30 amino acids).
1
In humans, insulin is made as one molecule and later folded into its active form. Meanwhile, in bacteria, A and B chain genes are cloned separately into plasmids and linked to the lacZ gene.
2
Bacteria produce a fusion protein (β-gal + insulin), which is then isolated.
3
Insulin is separated from β-gal, and the purified A and B chains are combined to form active insulin.
4
Recombinant insulin is purified for medical use, making production faster, safer, and cheaper for diabetic patients.

💊 Antibiotics
Microbes produce antibiotics, which are substances that inhibit or kill other microbes.
The discovery of penicillin by Alexander Fleming in 1928, when Penicillium notatum killed nearby Staphylococcus aureus, marked the start of the antibiotic era and led to the first widely used antibiotic in humans.

How Antibiotics Work

Antibiotics kill or stop bacteria by targeting:

  • Cell wall synthesis (e.g., Penicillin)
  • Protein synthesis (ribosome inhibitors)
  • DNA replication
  • Metabolic enzyme activity

Problem of Antibiotic Resistance

Overuse and misuse of antibiotics in humans and animals have led to antibiotic-resistant bacteria.

Resistant species examples:

  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis

New Frontiers in Antimicrobial Research

1. Recombinant bacteria that prevent disease
Streptococcus mutans engineered to reduce tooth decay.
2. Natural compounds from unusual sources
Licorice-based lollipops (UCLA) kill S. mutans.
🛡️ Vaccines
Vaccines
Vaccines prepare the immune system to recognize and fight microbes before infection.
Edward Jenner (1796) used cowpox to protect against smallpox, leading to vaccination; smallpox was eradicated in 1980.

Examples of Vaccines

Classic Vaccines

  • DPT (diphtheria, pertussis, tetanus) — bacterial toxins
  • MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) — viral infections
  • Polio vaccines (Salk & Sabin) — reduced poliomyelitis cases
Modern biotechnology: Recombinant DNA allows bacteria to produce antigens for vaccines safely.
  • • E. coli produces hepatitis B surface antigen
  • • Recombinant bacteria also produce antigens for HPV and other viral vaccines

Primer on Antibodies

The immune system protects the body by recognizing antigens (microbes, toxins, or microbial parts).
  • • B cells produce antibodies that attach to specific antigens, with help from T cells; some become plasma cells that release antibodies into body fluids.
  • • Some B cells become memory cells, allowing faster response upon future exposure — this is the basis of vaccination.
  • • Vaccines stimulate antibody production so the immune system can respond quickly to real infections.
  • • Biotechnology enhances immunity by producing recombinant antibodies and vaccines for diseases such as hepatitis, influenza, and HIV.

How Vaccines Are Made?

Vaccines are made using parts of a pathogen or weakened forms of the whole organism that can safely stimulate the immune system. These components act as antigens, triggering the body to produce antibodies and memory cells that provide protection against future infections.

Major Types of Vaccines

1. Subunit Vaccines

Contain specific proteins or lipids from a pathogen; can be produced using recombinant DNA technology.

e.g., hepatitis B, Novavax COVID-19

2. Attenuated Vaccines

Use live but weakened pathogens to produce strong and long-lasting immunity.

e.g., MMR, Sabin polio

3. Inactivated (Killed) Vaccines

Contain killed pathogens; often require booster shots.

e.g., Salk polio, rabies, Sinovac COVID-19

4. DNA-Based Vaccines

Use plasmids carrying antigen genes that cause cells to produce the antigen.

e.g., West Nile in horses, AstraZeneca COVID-19

5. mRNA Vaccines

Use messenger RNA to instruct cells to produce a harmless viral protein that triggers immunity.

e.g., Pfizer-BioNTech, Moderna

Recombinant Subunit Vaccines

Before recombinant DNA technology, subunit vaccines were made by growing pathogens and purifying their surface proteins, which carried contamination risks. Today, scientists clone antigen genes into plasmids and insert them into microbes like Escherichia coli or yeast, allowing large amounts of the antigenic protein to be safely produced and purified for vaccines.

Examples of Recombinant Vaccines

Hepatitis B Vaccine
Yeast-produced hepatitis B surface antigen
Gardasil (HPV Vaccine)
Protects against HPV 16, 18 (cervical cancer) and 6, 11 (genital warts); three doses. Shows how microbial biotechnology produces important vaccine proteins.

Booster Immunization

Some vaccines (especially inactivated types) do not provide lifelong protection. Antibody levels decline over time, and booster shots strengthen immune memory.
  • • DPT and tetanus — effective for about 10 years
  • • Influenza vaccine — requires annual immunization
